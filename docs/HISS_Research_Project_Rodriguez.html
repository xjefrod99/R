The figure in the introduction alongside the table of mortality rates show that Native Americans have a higher ratio of mortality rate to that of all races. Therefore, a higher risk of being affected by 14 out of the 16 most common causes of death. For some treatable diseases like diabetes, alcoholism, and chronic lower respiratory diseases, the mortality rate ratio is above 1. The first step to solving a medical disease is becoming informed about it, analyzing the progression of symptoms, and then treating the cause of the disease. Similarly, in this brief report I will present the causes of health disparities literature has analyzed, map clinical, behavioral, and social factors that contribute to health disparities, and offer an approach to begin to fix health disparities.

Given that a deficient health outcome is a problem seen to a larger extent in people who classify as Native American, both the State legislature and the federal government should focus their efforts on resolving the cause of the problem and then expenses that used to fund the symptoms of health disparities will decrease over the years. In this report, I will separate the social factors that create health disparities as upstream and downstream social determinants and analyze the available data for each factor. Upstream social determinants include “personal resources such as education and income and the social environments in which people live, work, study, and engage in recreational activities” (Woolf, Braveman, Satcher D, Saha S, Woolf SH, Braveman P, Link BG, Braveman PA, et al.). While downstream determinants “include medical care; environmental factors, such as air pollution; and health behaviors, such as smoking, seeking or forgoing medical care, and not adhering to treatment guidelines”(Woolf, Braveman, and others).

Sociologists experts have recognized that clinical, behavioral, and environmental factors influence health disparities. Clinical factors are those that affect access to medical care(Chokshi, Dhruv Khullar Dave A). People in lower income brackets are less likely to have health insurance, have access to medication, and primary care(Chokshi, Dhruv Khullar Dave A). Behavioral and environmental factors dictate how we interact as social creatures in a given environment. This is a different classification of factors that serves a similar purpose. In this report, I consider clinical factors as upstream social determinants and behavioral and environmental factors as downstream social determinants. The current capitalist society alludes to the illusion that more money will instantly result in a happier life. Believing this leads to people valuing their life status and measuring their happiness based on their income. In this case, income as the upstream factor directly affects behavior as a downstream factor. For this reason, it is possible that lower income Americans may feel high levels of societal pressure, and as a result may experience behavioral risk factors such as: smoking, substance abuse, or obesity. As of recently, we know that “people in families that earn less than $35,000 a year are three times more likely to smoke as those in families with an annual income of more than $100,000,” which shows that lower income people are not dealing with chronic stress well(Chokshi, Dhruv Khullar Dave A). Households with lower income do not have the same resources as that of households with a higher annual income as they have better access to seeing a counselor, psychologist, or psychiatrist. This is another instance of income as an upstream factor influencing access to medical care. Moreover, low-income communities are more likely to turn to fast food services on a regular basis because they are cheaper, instead of healthier(and more expensive) restaurant food which in the long term results in higher obesity and chronic disease rates (Chokshi, Dhruv Khullar Dave A). Hence, a higher income is associated with healthier nutrition.

Given that upstream determinants such as income and the level and quality of education affect downstream determinants such as access to medical care, environmental factors, and health behaviors, we should examine the available data to comprehend the health disparities affecting minorities.

Upstream Determinant: Income

To analyze how health disparities form, looking at wage gaps between different race groups and counties is essential. Given that the U.S. was founded by and is mostly inhabited by white Americans, looking at wage differences in race gives us insight into systemic problems. Having a higher income naturally puts people in a higher social class, and this is associated with a longer life expectancy as they have access to more resources. Income is considered an upstream determinant as it yields a type of income-related activities, tools, and education.

Upstream Determinant: Education

A formidable primary and secondary education prepares students to be adequately prepared for societal integration. That includes having a plan of action after high school graduation, for some that means going to college, trade school, or joining the “real” world by  working multiple jobs. The Native American community faces the challenge of  not having enough funding for their education system. Since school funding is a combination of federal, state, and local money, counties that have a lower median income and include reservations have less funding("Public School Funding in the United States." Wikipedia). This is the case as inhabitants of reservations do not pay property taxes and usually these taxes fund public schools. Moreover, the federal government only contributes  8% to public schools("Public School Funding in the United States." Wikipedia) .This means that by the time Native American students graduate high school, they did not receive nearly the same standard of education students of a different country would have received. Additionally, this upstream factor is combined with a low income bracket means that Native American students without access to private tutors or expensive books that prepare students for standardized tests or AP tests decreases the chances of Native American high school graduates attending a college that offers them a hefty financial aid package. Interestingly, rural isolation which is common in Native schools leads to low teacher salaries, high levels of poverty, difference in culture/language taught, and it is hard to have high quality teachers stay at the public school for more than a few years ("American Indians/Alaska Natives: Education Issues." NEA. ). Moreover, the presence of Native Americans and Alaska Native in the undergraduate and graduate populations are 1% and less than 1% respectively such that postsecondary research efforts leave out their data due to small sample sizes ("Factsheets." PNPI). The available data does show that approximately “17% of Native American students continue their education after high school compared to 60% of the U.S. population” ("Factsheets." PNPI).

Aside from primary and secondary education affecting whether Native students continue their education after high school, a low standard of education naturally affects the type of jobs these students will have in the future. Therefore, the current system reinforces the way in which income and education affect each other. Furthermore, people that are more academically prepared often have a higher income.

Downstream Determinant: Health Insurance

The people who live in reservations, as noted by Figures 4, 8, and 12 have a lower median income than the median income of each state. Similarly, Native Americans are one of the racial minorities that have one of the lowest median income per state as noted by Figures 2, 6, and 10. The type of jobs Native American possess are usually in agriculture, community based, technical based, or dependent on tourism. Moreover, the census considers people uninsured if they “had no reported health coverage, or those whose only health coverage was Indian Health Service” (Bureau, US Census. "Code Lists, Definitions, and Accuracy." The United States Census Bureau.). This is probably the Indian Health Insurance is not an insurance program, it only provides state services such as “Temporary Assistance for Needy Families (TANF), Supplemental Security Income (SSI), the Food Stamp Program and the Low Income Heating and Energy Assistance Program (LIHEAP)” ("Benefits and Service." U.S. Department of the Interior). Therefore, people who are not uninsured (not insured by their employers or only have Indian Health Insurance) do not have the same resources to pay for regular visits to a primary case physician, mental health specialist, or buy medications.

Downstream Determinant: Health Behaviors

        As upstream determinants influence one another and directly affect downstream determinants, behavioral and environmental factors are the last ones to contribute to health disparities. How every person chooses to interact with each other and spend their leisure time varies greatly. Some habits are healthier than others in the long run. For instance, substance use if used with moderation is not that harmful, nonetheless substance abuse and addiction has long term health concerns. Alcohol abuse can result in alcohol induced deaths, chronic liver disease, and even suicide. Abusing cannabis or cigarettes can result in chronic respiratory deaths, strokes, or cardiac diseases in the long term.

Moreover, combining high levels of a county population living below poverty level with substance abuse can lead to the prevalence of crime in a community to increase. Crime is a downstream determinant of health disparities as it affects the whole community by inciting fear and stress. Higher levels of cortisol during stress are associated with more anxiety or depression, which is assumed to have deleterious effects on the physical health. Conclusion

To begin to resolve the health disparities that exist, the federal government must focus more on upstream determinants factors. First, the federal government should increase their contribution to public school on reservations from 8% to at least 10%. A better quality of education will increase the learning of Native children. Moreover, on a state level there should be sufficient transportation from each reservation to other counties multiple times during the day. Additionally, both the state and federal government should increase education opportunities for high school graduates, in the form of twice as many scholarships for technical schools,public, and private colleges. Increasing the standard of education will result in the following generation to have better opportunities for higher paying jobs. Also, more jobs should be created in counties which have more than 10% of the population living below the poverty level. Furthermore, I propose that the Indian Health Insurance program actually provides health insurance to Natives as right now the naming is a contradiction to the benefits it provides. These steps will not fix health disparities immediately, but by tackling the root of the problem(upstream and downstream determinants) these health disparities will decrease with time.
